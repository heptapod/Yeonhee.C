Project Concept & Brief Response

Project Overview

Wish You Were Here is an affordable, accessible AR application designed to provide immediate mental health support for individuals with PTSD, CPTSD, and sensory-sensitive neurodivergent conditions. The project responds to critical gaps in current mental health services by offering a tangible, hybrid XR experience that combines virtual companionship with physical comfort.

Core Innovation: Users interact with a semi-transparent 2D animated companion overlaid on a physical object (heated water bottle) through their smartphone camera, creating a "hybrid reality" that provides both visual and tactile comfort without requiring expensive VR equipment.

Problem Statement

Traditional mental health services often fail individuals experiencing trauma-related hypervigilance, panic attacks, and social isolation—particularly during crisis periods when immediate support is needed but emergency services are inappropriate. Current VR solutions are expensive, inaccessible, and often overwhelming for trauma survivors.

Solution Approach

This project develops an affordable alternative using existing technology (smartphones + common household items) to create safe, controlled therapeutic experiences. The design prioritizes accessibility, affordability, and scalability while maintaining therapeutic efficacy through evidence-based approaches to trauma-informed care.

Related Material & Research Foundation

Core Research Influences

Harry Harlow's Contact Comfort Studies (1958): Foundational research demonstrating that physical comfort and contact are fundamental to emotional regulation and stress reduction, informing the tactile component of this design.
Temple Grandin's Deep Touch Pressure Research: Evidence that controlled pressure and tactile stimulation can significantly reduce anxiety and hypervigilance in neurodivergent individuals, directly influencing the heated water bottle interaction design.
Animal-Assisted Therapy Literature: Research demonstrating the therapeutic benefits of safe, non-judgmental companionship in trauma recovery, providing the conceptual framework for the virtual companion design.

Technical Inspiration

MIT Media Lab's TREE Project: A 4D hybrid VR experience that combines virtual visuals with synchronized physical effects (wind, temperature) to create heightened immersion. This project inspired the hybrid approach of combining visual AR with physical tactile interaction.

Previous Prototype: An earlier VR version of "Wish You Were Here" explored similar concepts using expensive headset technology. This current iteration reimagines the experience for broader accessibility and real-world implementation.

E

User Journey & Experience Design

User Flow Overview

Step 1: Setup (2 minutes)

User attaches custom markers to water bottle and hand, fills bottle with warm water, and launches the AR application on their smartphone.

Step 2: Companion Interaction (5-30 minutes)

Semi-transparent 2D animated companion appears overlaid on the physical water bottle. User engages in gentle tactile interaction while the visual companion responds with calming animations and presence.

Step 3: Regulation & Closure (2-5 minutes)

Session gradually concludes with grounding exercises, leaving user in a regulated state with the option to repeat as needed.

Accessibility Features

The experience is designed to work on older smartphones with minimal processing requirements, uses simple marker-based tracking to reduce computational load, and requires only common household items (water bottle, markers) to ensure broad accessibility regardless of economic status.

Additional Feature: Stereoscopic AR Enhancement

Innovation Beyond Standard AR

The additional feature implements a stereoscopic viewing mode that transforms the smartphone AR experience into a more immersive, hands-free solution. This addresses a key limitation of handheld AR for therapeutic use—the need to hold the device while seeking comfort.

Technical Implementation

A modified Google Cardboard case with an open camera section allows users to wear the device like VR goggles while maintaining video pass-through capability. The screen splits into left and right eye views, each rendering slightly different camera angles to create natural depth perception.

// Stereoscopic rendering implementation public class StereoscopicRenderer : MonoBehaviour { [SerializeField] private Camera leftEyeCamera; [SerializeField] private Camera rightEyeCamera; [SerializeField] private float eyeSeparation = 0.064f; // Average IPD void Update() { Vector3 leftPos = transform.position - transform.right * (eyeSeparation / 2); Vector3 rightPos = transform.position + transform.right * (eyeSeparation / 2); leftEyeCamera.transform.position = leftPos; rightEyeCamera.transform.position = rightPos; } }

User Experience Impact

This enhancement allows users to fully embrace the physical comfort object while maintaining visual connection with the virtual companion, creating a more natural and therapeutically effective interaction paradigm.

I

Reflection

Meeting Project Aims

The project successfully addresses its core objective of creating an accessible, affordable mental health support tool for trauma survivors. By combining lived experience insights with evidence-based therapeutic approaches, "Wish You Were Here" demonstrates how XR technology can be democratized and made relevant to real-world mental health needs.

The hybrid approach—combining AR visuals with physical tactile interaction—represents a novel contribution to therapeutic technology design, potentially offering more effective comfort than purely digital solutions while maintaining the scalability of digital interventions.

Challenges & Limitations

Solo Development Complexity: Managing the entire pipeline from research through technical implementation while navigating personal CPTSD symptoms presented significant challenges. This experience reinforced the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration for future iterations.

N=1 Research Limitations: As this solution is currently based on the developer's lived experience, broader validation is essential. The sensitive nature of trauma research requires careful ethical consideration and collaboration with clinical professionals for responsible scaling.

Technical Constraints: Balancing accessibility (working on older devices) with advanced features (stereoscopic rendering) required careful optimization and compromise in visual fidelity.

Future Development & Impact Potential

Clinical Validation: Next steps include collaboration with trauma therapists and clinical researchers to validate effectiveness through controlled studies with appropriate ethical oversight.

Scalability Vision: The low-cost, smartphone-based approach positions this solution for potential integration into healthcare systems like the NHS, where budget constraints often limit access to innovative therapeutic tools.

Community Impact: By demonstrating that lived experience can drive meaningful technological innovation, this project contributes to broader conversations about inclusive design and the importance of centering affected communities in solution development.
